# Sentence Review Framework

## Review Process Overview

This framework provides a systematic approach to reviewing and improving writing at the sentence level. The review follows three phases:

1. **Initial Assessment** - Score the text and provide overall evaluation
2. **Systematic Analysis** - Identify specific issues sentence by sentence
3. **Structured Output** - Present findings with multiple rewrite options

## Phase 1: Initial Assessment

### Quality Scoring Rubric

Evaluate the text on four dimensions using a 1-10 scale. Be honest but constructive—most writing falls in the 5-7 range.

#### Clarity (1-10)

How easily can readers understand the intended meaning?

**1-3: Poor**
- Confusing or ambiguous meaning
- Reader must reread sentences multiple times
- Unclear relationships between ideas
- Buried subjects and verbs

**4-6: Fair**
- Mostly understandable but requires effort
- Some ambiguity or unnecessary complexity
- Occasional confusion about meaning
- Could be more direct

**7-9: Good**
- Clear and easy to understand
- Meaning is unambiguous
- Logical flow between ideas
- Direct communication

**10: Excellent**
- Crystal clear with no possibility of misunderstanding
- Effortless reading experience
- Perfect clarity of thought
- Every sentence lands precisely

#### Concision (1-10)

How efficiently does the text convey its meaning?

**1-3: Poor**
- Extremely wordy with significant redundancy
- Excessive filler words and phrases
- Many weak verb + noun constructions
- Could be cut by 40%+ without losing meaning

**4-6: Fair**
- Some unnecessary words throughout
- Occasional redundancy
- Could be tightened noticeably
- Room for 20-30% reduction

**7-9: Good**
- Efficient with minimal excess
- Few unnecessary words
- Mostly strong, direct constructions
- Perhaps 5-10% could be trimmed

**10: Excellent**
- Every word earns its place
- Zero redundancy
- Perfectly economical
- Cannot be shortened without losing meaning

#### Word Choice (1-10)

How precise, appropriate, and effective is the vocabulary?

**1-3: Poor**
- Vague or imprecise vocabulary ("thing," "stuff," "get")
- Inappropriate formality for audience
- Clichés and tired phrases throughout
- Weak or generic verbs

**4-6: Fair**
- Adequate but could be more precise
- Some vague words could be replaced
- Occasional inappropriate formality
- Mix of weak and strong verbs

**7-9: Good**
- Strong, appropriate word choices
- Precise vocabulary
- Good verb strength
- Suitable tone for audience

**10: Excellent**
- Perfect precision and vividness
- Every word exactly right for context
- Powerful verbs throughout
- Ideal tone and register

#### Rhythm & Flow (1-10)

How well does the text use sentence length variation and natural cadence?

**1-3: Poor**
- Monotonous sentence length (all similar)
- Awkward or unnatural constructions
- Plodding or choppy rhythm
- Uncomfortable to read aloud

**4-6: Fair**
- Some variation but could be better
- Generally acceptable rhythm
- Occasional awkwardness
- Readable but not engaging

**7-9: Good**
- Good sentence length variation
- Natural, flowing rhythm
- Pleasant to read aloud
- Engaging cadence

**10: Excellent**
- Masterful variation and pacing
- Musical quality to the prose
- Perfect rhythm for content and tone
- Effortlessly engaging

### Overall Score Calculation

**Overall Score = Average of four dimensions**

Round to one decimal place (e.g., 7.3, 6.8)

### Overall Assessment Categories

Based on the overall score, classify the writing:

- **8.5-10.0: Excellent** - Strong writing with minor refinement opportunities
- **7.0-8.4: Good** - Solid writing with some improvements recommended
- **5.0-6.9: Fair** - Multiple issues to address across several areas
- **Below 5.0: Needs Significant Revision** - Fundamental issues requiring substantial work

### Writing the Assessment Summary

After scoring, write a 2-3 sentence summary that:
1. Acknowledges strengths (if any)
2. Identifies primary areas needing improvement
3. Sets expectations for the detailed analysis

**Example summaries:**

**For 7.5/10 text:**
"The writing is generally clear and professional with good word choice. The main opportunities for improvement lie in concision—several sentences contain unnecessary words that could be trimmed. Additionally, varying sentence length more would improve the rhythm and engagement."

**For 5.5/10 text:**
"The text communicates its core message but struggles with clarity and concision. Many sentences contain weak verb constructions and filler phrases that dilute the impact. Significant tightening and restructuring would strengthen the writing considerably."

**For 8.8/10 text:**
"This is strong, professional writing with excellent clarity and rhythm. Minor refinements in a few spots could polish it further, but the text already demonstrates sophisticated sentence craft."

## Phase 2: Systematic Analysis

### Sentence-by-Sentence Review Process

#### Step 1: First Pass (Intuitive)

Read through the entire text and flag sentences that:
- Feel weak or awkward
- Cause you to stumble or reread
- Are overly long or confusing
- Sound unnatural when read aloud
- Contain obvious issues (passive voice, weak verbs, etc.)

Mark these for deeper analysis.

#### Step 2: Systematic Analysis (Checklist)

For each flagged sentence, work through this checklist:

**Clarity Checks:**
- [ ] Is the meaning clear and unambiguous?
- [ ] Is the subject clearly identified and positioned early?
- [ ] Does the sentence contain one main idea, or is it trying to do too much?
- [ ] Is the sentence structure unnecessarily complex?
- [ ] Are there buried verbs or subjects?

**Concision Checks:**
- [ ] Are there unnecessary filler words or phrases?
- [ ] Can any phrases be tightened or eliminated?
- [ ] Are there redundancies (saying the same thing twice)?
- [ ] Is there a weak verb + noun that could become a strong verb?
- [ ] Are there qualifiers that add no value ("very," "quite," "rather")?

**Word Choice Checks:**
- [ ] Are the words precise, or are there vague terms ("thing," "get," "good")?
- [ ] Are verbs strong and vivid, or weak and generic?
- [ ] Is the formality level appropriate for the audience?
- [ ] Are there clichés or overused phrases?
- [ ] Is jargon used appropriately (or at all)?

**Rhythm Checks:**
- [ ] Is the sentence length varied from surrounding sentences?
- [ ] Does it flow naturally when read aloud?
- [ ] Are there awkward constructions or word orders?
- [ ] Is punctuation used effectively for pacing?
- [ ] Does the sentence contribute to overall cadence?

#### Step 3: Categorize the Issue

Identify the primary problem type for each flagged sentence:
- Weak verb construction
- Conjunction chain (sentence killer)
- Buried subject/verb
- Vague word choice
- Passive voice overuse
- Redundancy
- Inappropriate formality
- Monotonous rhythm
- Other (describe specifically)

This helps explain the issue clearly in the output.

### Identifying Strengths

While the focus is on improvement, also note:
- Particularly well-crafted sentences
- Effective use of specific techniques
- Strong word choices or rhythm

Include 1-2 examples in the "Strengths Worth Noting" section if applicable.

## Common Sentence Problems Catalog

This catalog provides examples of the most frequent sentence issues with multiple rewrite options. Use these as reference when analyzing text and generating rewrites.

### Problem Type 1: Weak Verb + Noun Construction

**Pattern:** Using weak verbs (make, do, have, give, take, provide) with nouns instead of strong verbs

**Common examples:**
- "make a decision" → "decide"
- "give consideration to" → "consider"
- "have a discussion" → "discuss"
- "conduct an analysis" → "analyze"
- "provide assistance" → "assist" or "help"

**Example Analysis:**

❌ **Weak:** "We will make a determination about the budget after we conduct an analysis of the costs."

✅ **Option A (Recommended):** "We will determine the budget after analyzing the costs."
- **Improves:** Replaces two weak verb+noun constructions with strong verbs, cutting 7 words (from 17 to 10)
- **Principle:** Concision - weak verb elimination

✅ **Option B:** "We'll analyze costs before determining the budget."
- **Improves:** Stronger verbs plus reordered for logical flow, most concise at 8 words
- **Principle:** Concision + Clarity - logical sequence

✅ **Option C:** "After analyzing costs, we will determine the budget."
- **Improves:** Strong verbs with time relationship front-loaded for clarity
- **Principle:** Concision + Clarity - temporal emphasis

---

### Problem Type 2: Conjunction Chain (Sentence Killer)

**Pattern:** Overusing "and," "but," or "or" to string multiple ideas together

**Red flags:**
- Three or more "and"s in one sentence
- Sentence runs past 25 words due to conjunctions
- Reader loses track of the point

**Example Analysis:**

❌ **Weak:** "The product is fast and reliable and easy to use and it has great reviews."

✅ **Option A (Recommended):** "The product is fast, reliable, and easy to use. It has great reviews."
- **Improves:** Splits into two sentences with proper list punctuation, clearer structure
- **Principle:** Clarity & Rhythm - avoid conjunction chains

✅ **Option B:** "The product is fast, reliable, and well-reviewed. It's also easy to use."
- **Improves:** Consolidates "great reviews" into adjective, reorganizes emphasis
- **Principle:** Clarity & Word Choice - concise adjective use

✅ **Option C:** "The product combines speed and reliability with ease of use—plus great reviews."
- **Improves:** Uses stronger verb "combines" and em-dash for emphasis on reviews
- **Principle:** Rhythm - punctuation for effect

---

### Problem Type 3: Buried Subject/Verb

**Pattern:** Main subject and verb hidden in the middle or delayed by introductory phrases

**Red flags:**
- Sentences starting with "In order to," "In light of," "Due to the fact that"
- "There is/are" constructions
- Subject appears after 8+ words

**Example Analysis:**

❌ **Weak:** "In order to achieve our quarterly goals, it is necessary for the team to focus on key priorities."

✅ **Option A (Recommended):** "The team must focus on key priorities to achieve our quarterly goals."
- **Improves:** Front-loads subject and verb, eliminates filler "in order to" and "it is necessary," cuts from 18 to 14 words
- **Principle:** Clarity - direct subject-verb structure

✅ **Option B:** "To achieve our quarterly goals, the team must focus on key priorities."
- **Improves:** Begins with purpose, still direct, same 14-word count
- **Principle:** Clarity - purpose-first structure

✅ **Option C:** "Focus is necessary: the team must achieve our quarterly goals through key priorities."
- **Improves:** Most dramatic, emphasizes action, uses colon for anticipation
- **Principle:** Grammar for Effect - emphasis through structure

---

### Problem Type 4: Vague Word Choice

**Pattern:** Using generic words when specific ones would be clearer

**Common vague words:**
- thing, stuff, item
- good, bad, nice
- get, make, do
- area, aspect, issue

**Example Analysis:**

❌ **Weak:** "The thing didn't work very well in that situation."

✅ **Option A (Recommended):** "The device malfunctioned during testing."
- **Improves:** Replaces "thing" with "device," "didn't work" with "malfunctioned," "that situation" with "testing," removes useless "very well," cuts from 9 to 5 words
- **Principle:** Word Choice - precision + Concision

✅ **Option B:** "The software crashed during the demonstration."
- **Improves:** Even more specific (software vs. device), vivid verb "crashed," specific context
- **Principle:** Word Choice - precision and vividness

✅ **Option C:** "The feature failed to load properly."
- **Improves:** Specific about what (feature) and how it failed (load failure)
- **Principle:** Word Choice - technical precision

---

### Problem Type 5: Monotonous Rhythm

**Pattern:** All sentences similar length, creating boring, plodding cadence

**Red flags:**
- Five+ consecutive sentences of similar length (all 12-18 words, for example)
- No short punchy sentences for emphasis
- No longer flowing sentences for development
- Feels repetitive when read aloud

**Example Analysis:**

❌ **Weak:** "Our product is innovative. Our team is experienced. Our customers are satisfied. Our prices are competitive."
(All 4-5 words, monotonous)

✅ **Option A (Recommended):** "Our product is innovative. With an experienced team and satisfied customers behind us, we're positioned for growth. Our prices? Competitive."
- **Improves:** Varies length (5 words, 14 words, 2 words), uses question for emphasis
- **Principle:** Rhythm - varied sentence length

✅ **Option B:** "Innovation drives our product. Experience defines our team. Satisfaction marks our customers. Together with competitive pricing, these strengths position us for growth."
- **Improves:** Parallel structure variation (short, short, short, longer), builds to conclusion
- **Principle:** Rhythm - parallel structure with variation

✅ **Option C:** "Three strengths set us apart: an innovative product, an experienced team, and satisfied customers. Add competitive pricing, and you have a winning combination."
- **Improves:** Combines into two longer sentences with list structure, more sophisticated
- **Principle:** Rhythm + Grammar for Effect - colon use

---

### Problem Type 6: Passive Voice Overuse

**Pattern:** Passive construction when active would be clearer and more concise

**Red flags:**
- Multiple "was/were" + past participle constructions
- Actor mentioned after "by" or hidden entirely
- Unnecessarily indirect or wordy

**Example Analysis:**

❌ **Weak:** "The report was written by the team and it was submitted yesterday to management."

✅ **Option A (Recommended):** "The team wrote and submitted the report to management yesterday."
- **Improves:** Active voice, clearer actor, more direct, cuts from 15 to 11 words
- **Principle:** Clarity + Concision - active voice preference

✅ **Option B:** "The team submitted the report to management yesterday."
- **Improves:** Most concise, combines "wrote and submitted" into just "submitted" (implied writing), 9 words
- **Principle:** Concision - eliminate implied information

✅ **Option C:** "Yesterday, the team wrote the report and submitted it to management."
- **Improves:** Active voice with time emphasis first, slightly more formal
- **Principle:** Clarity - temporal sequence emphasis

---

### Problem Type 7: Redundancy

**Pattern:** Saying the same thing twice or using redundant modifiers

**Common redundancies:**
- "collaborate together" (collaborate means together)
- "past history" (history is always past)
- "advance planning" (planning is always in advance)
- "end result" (result is always at the end)
- "future goals" (goals are always future)

**Example Analysis:**

❌ **Weak:** "We need to collaborate together as a team to achieve our future goals ahead of us."

✅ **Option A (Recommended):** "We need to collaborate to achieve our goals."
- **Improves:** Removes "together" (redundant), "as a team" (implied), "future" (redundant), "ahead of us" (redundant), cuts from 16 to 8 words—50% reduction
- **Principle:** Concision - eliminate redundancy

✅ **Option B:** "Team collaboration will help us achieve our goals."
- **Improves:** Converts verb to noun form, equally concise alternative, 8 words
- **Principle:** Concision - structural variation

✅ **Option C:** "Achieving our goals requires collaboration."
- **Improves:** Most concise, flips emphasis to goals first, 6 words
- **Principle:** Concision + Clarity - goal-focused

---

### Problem Type 8: Inappropriate Formality

**Pattern:** Using unnecessarily formal or pretentious language for the audience/context

**Red flags:**
- "utilize" instead of "use"
- "facilitate" instead of "help"
- "implement" when "start" or "do" is clearer
- Jargon in contexts where plain language works better

**Example Analysis:**

❌ **Weak:** "We will utilize our available resources to facilitate the implementation of the optimization strategy."

✅ **Option A (Recommended):** "We'll use our resources to help implement the improvement strategy."
- **Improves:** Replaces "utilize" with "use," "facilitate" with "help," "optimization" with "improvement," more natural tone, cuts from 14 to 10 words
- **Principle:** Word Choice - appropriate formality

✅ **Option B:** "We'll use our resources to improve our strategy."
- **Improves:** Most direct, eliminates "facilitate the implementation" entirely, 8 words
- **Principle:** Concision + Word Choice - directness

✅ **Option C:** "Our resources will help us improve our strategy."
- **Improves:** Subjects are "resources," slightly different emphasis but equally clear, 8 words
- **Principle:** Clarity - varied structure option

## Guidelines for Generating Multiple Rewrite Options

### When to Provide 2 vs. 3 Options

**Provide 2 options when:**
- The fix is straightforward with limited valid alternatives
- The two options represent clearly different approaches (one conservative, one bold)
- Additional options would be too similar to be useful

**Provide 3 options when:**
- Multiple valid approaches exist with different emphases
- The context could favor different stylistic choices
- There's a conservative fix, a moderate fix, and a creative fix
- Different audiences might prefer different versions

### Differentiating Your Options

Make sure each option offers something distinct:

**Option A (Recommended):** Usually the most straightforward improvement
- Addresses the primary issue directly
- Maintains original meaning most closely
- Safest, most conservative change
- Best for general audiences

**Option B:** Alternative approach or different emphasis
- May reorganize structure more significantly
- Might shift emphasis to a different element
- Could be more concise or more elaborate depending on context
- Offers stylistic variation

**Option C:** Creative, bold, or context-specific alternative
- May combine multiple improvements at once
- Could be more dramatic in tone or structure
- Might work better for specific audiences or purposes
- Shows range of possibilities

### Explaining Improvements Effectively

For each option, always specify:

**1. What Changed (be specific and quantify when possible)**
- "Cuts from 18 to 12 words"
- "Replaces weak verb 'make' with strong verb 'decide'"
- "Front-loads subject for immediate clarity"
- "Splits one complex sentence into two clear ones"

**2. Why It's Better (concrete benefit)**
- "More direct and easier to understand"
- "Stronger verb creates more impact"
- "Better rhythm through varied sentence length"
- "Eliminates ambiguity about who is responsible"

**3. Which Principle (reference principles.md)**
- "Concision: Weak verb elimination"
- "Clarity: Direct subject-verb structure"
- "Word Choice: Precision and vividness"
- "Rhythm: Sentence length variation"
- "Grammar for Effect: Strategic punctuation"

### Example of Well-Differentiated Options

❌ **Original:** "The team is currently in the process of making a decision regarding the best approach to take for solving the problem at hand."

✅ **Option A (Conservative):** "The team is deciding the best approach to solve the problem."
- **Improves:** Eliminates filler phrases "currently in the process of" and "at hand," simplifies "making a decision" to "deciding," "regarding" to implicit, cuts from 22 to 11 words
- **Principle:** Concision - eliminate filler and weak verb constructions

✅ **Option B (Moderate):** "The team is deciding how to solve the problem."
- **Improves:** Most concise version, "best approach to take" becomes "how," 8 words total
- **Principle:** Concision - radical simplification while preserving meaning

✅ **Option C (Creative):** "The team is choosing a solution."
- **Improves:** Reframes from "deciding approach to solve problem" to "choosing solution," ultra-concise at 5 words, shifts emphasis to outcome
- **Principle:** Concision + Word Choice - outcome-focused framing

Notice how each option progressively simplifies while maintaining the core meaning, giving the writer real choices based on their preferred level of concision and emphasis.

## Summary Checklist

Before finalizing your review, verify:

- [ ] Overall scores provided for all four dimensions
- [ ] Overall score calculated as average
- [ ] Assessment category assigned (Excellent/Good/Fair/Needs Revision)
- [ ] Summary paragraph written (2-3 sentences)
- [ ] Number of issues stated
- [ ] Each issue includes location, problem description, current sentence
- [ ] Each issue has 2-3 distinct rewrite options
- [ ] Each option has "Improves" and "Principle Applied" explanations
- [ ] Explanations are specific and helpful, not generic
- [ ] Strengths section included if applicable
- [ ] Output format matches template exactly

Remember: The goal is to help the writer understand not just what to change, but **why** each change improves the writing and **how** different approaches offer different benefits.
