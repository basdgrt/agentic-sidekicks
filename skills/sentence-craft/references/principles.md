# Sentence Craft Principles

## Overview

Great writing is built sentence by sentence. This guide synthesizes principles from June Casagrande's "It Was the Best of Sentences, It Was the Worst of Sentences" with proven sentence craft best practices. The focus is on four key areas: clarity and concision, word choice, rhythm and flow, and grammar for effect.

The sentence is the fundamental unit of writing. Too many writers overlook this, leading to manuscripts that never get published and writing careers that never begin. By mastering sentence-level craft, you transform ordinary writing into compelling prose.

## 1. Clarity & Concision

### Sentence Killers to Eliminate

**Conjunction Chains ("Conjunctions That Kill")**

Overusing "and," "but," or "or" to string multiple ideas together weakens sentences and creates monotonous rhythm.

❌ **Weak:** "The product is innovative and reliable and affordable and it comes with great support."

✅ **Strong:** "The product is innovative, reliable, and affordable. It comes with great support."

✅ **Alternative:** "The product combines innovation and reliability with affordability—plus great support."

**Why it's better:** Breaking the chain creates clearer structure and better rhythm. The first option separates ideas; the second uses punctuation for emphasis.

---

❌ **Weak:** "We need to finish the report and submit it to the client and make sure they approve it and then we can move forward."

✅ **Strong:** "We need to finish the report and submit it to the client. Once they approve it, we can move forward."

✅ **Alternative:** "Three steps remain: finish the report, get client approval, and move forward."

**Why it's better:** Reduces cognitive load by breaking complex sequence into manageable parts.

---

**Filler Words and Phrases**

These add length without meaning:
- "in order to" → "to"
- "due to the fact that" → "because"
- "at this point in time" → "now"
- "it is important to note that" → (delete entirely)
- "the reason why is because" → "because"

❌ **Weak:** "In order to achieve success, it is important to note that we need to focus on our core competencies."

✅ **Strong:** "To succeed, we must focus on our core competencies."

**Why it's better:** Removes 8 unnecessary words while maintaining full meaning.

---

**Passive Voice Overuse**

Passive constructions often bury the actor and weaken sentences.

❌ **Weak:** "The decision was made by the committee that the proposal would be rejected."

✅ **Strong:** "The committee rejected the proposal."

**Why it's better:** Direct, clear, and 60% shorter.

---

❌ **Weak:** "Mistakes were made and corrections will be implemented by the team."

✅ **Strong:** "The team will correct its mistakes."

✅ **Alternative:** "We made mistakes. We'll fix them."

**Why it's better:** Accepts responsibility clearly and shows action.

---

**Buried Subjects and Verbs**

When the main subject and verb hide in the middle of a sentence, readers work harder.

❌ **Weak:** "In light of recent developments in the market, there is a need for the company to reassess its strategy."

✅ **Strong:** "The company must reassess its strategy given recent market developments."

✅ **Alternative:** "Recent market developments require strategic reassessment."

**Why it's better:** Front-loads the actor and action for immediate clarity.

### Concision Techniques

**Weak Verb + Noun → Strong Verb**

This is one of the most common sentence killers. Writers use weak verbs (make, do, have, give, take, provide) with nouns when a single strong verb would suffice.

Common patterns:
- "make a decision" → "decide"
- "give consideration to" → "consider"
- "have a discussion about" → "discuss"
- "provide assistance to" → "assist" or "help"
- "take action on" → "act on"
- "make an improvement to" → "improve"
- "conduct an analysis of" → "analyze"
- "make an assumption" → "assume"
- "reach a conclusion" → "conclude"
- "give approval to" → "approve"

❌ **Weak:** "We will make a determination about the budget after we conduct an analysis of the costs."

✅ **Strong:** "We will determine the budget after analyzing costs."

**Why it's better:** Cuts 7 words while strengthening both verbs.

---

❌ **Weak:** "The team gave consideration to the proposal and made the recommendation that we proceed."

✅ **Strong:** "The team considered the proposal and recommended we proceed."

**Why it's better:** More direct and professional.

---

**Eliminate Redundancies**

Many common phrases say the same thing twice:

- "past history" → "history"
- "future plans" → "plans"
- "end result" → "result"
- "advance planning" → "planning"
- "collaborate together" → "collaborate"
- "consensus of opinion" → "consensus"
- "each and every" → "each" or "every"
- "first and foremost" → "first" or "foremost"
- "various different" → "various" or "different"
- "completely finished" → "finished"

❌ **Weak:** "We need to collaborate together as a team to create an advance plan for our future goals ahead."

✅ **Strong:** "We need to collaborate as a team to plan for our goals."

✅ **Alternative:** "Team collaboration will help us reach our goals."

**Why it's better:** Eliminates 6 redundant words.

---

**Remove Qualifiers That Add No Value**

Words like "very," "quite," "rather," "somewhat," "really," and "actually" often weaken rather than strengthen:

❌ **Weak:** "The solution is very effective and quite efficient."

✅ **Strong:** "The solution is effective and efficient."

✅ **Alternative:** "The solution is highly effective and efficient." (when emphasis is truly needed)

**Why it's better:** Confident assertions are stronger than hedged ones.

---

**Tighten Phrases**

Many multi-word phrases can become single words:

- "a majority of" → "most"
- "a number of" → "several" or "many"
- "at the present time" → "now" or "currently"
- "in the event that" → "if"
- "in spite of the fact that" → "although"
- "with regard to" → "regarding" or "about"
- "for the purpose of" → "to"
- "in the process of" → (often deletable)
- "is able to" → "can"

❌ **Weak:** "A number of employees have expressed concerns with regard to the new policy."

✅ **Strong:** "Several employees have concerns about the new policy."

**Why it's better:** Clearer and more direct.

## 2. Word Choice

### Precision: From Vague to Specific

**Vague Nouns → Specific Nouns**

Generic words force readers to guess your meaning:

- "thing" → "device, tool, method, approach, system"
- "stuff" → be specific to context
- "issue" → "problem, challenge, concern, bug, error"
- "area" → "region, field, department, aspect, section"
- "aspect" → name the specific aspect
- "situation" → describe the actual situation

❌ **Weak:** "The thing didn't work well in that situation."

✅ **Strong:** "The device malfunctioned during testing."

✅ **Alternative:** "The software crashed during the demo."

**Why it's better:** Readers know exactly what happened and when.

---

**Vague Adjectives → Specific Adjectives**

- "good" → "effective, beneficial, valuable, profitable, reliable"
- "bad" → "ineffective, harmful, flawed, broken, unreliable"
- "big" → "large, substantial, significant, major, extensive"
- "small" → "minor, limited, modest, slight, minimal"
- "nice" → "pleasant, helpful, thoughtful, attractive, welcoming"

❌ **Weak:** "We had a good meeting with positive results."

✅ **Strong:** "We had a productive meeting with actionable results."

✅ **Alternative:** "Our strategic meeting produced three concrete initiatives."

**Why it's better:** Specific adjectives convey actual meaning.

---

**Generic Verbs → Vivid Verbs**

Weak verbs like "get," "make," "do," "have," "go," and "said" often miss opportunities for precision:

**"Get" alternatives:**
- get (obtain) → obtain, acquire, receive, secure, gain
- get (understand) → understand, grasp, comprehend
- get (become) → become, grow, turn
- get (arrive) → arrive, reach

**"Go" alternatives:**
- go (travel) → travel, journey, head
- go (leave) → leave, depart, exit
- go (proceed) → proceed, continue, advance
- go (work) → work, function, operate

**"Said" alternatives:**
- said (inform) → stated, explained, noted, mentioned
- said (argue) → argued, contended, insisted, maintained
- said (whisper) → whispered, murmured, confided
- said (shout) → shouted, exclaimed, declared, announced

❌ **Weak:** "The CEO said the company would go into new markets and get better results."

✅ **Strong:** "The CEO announced the company would expand into new markets and achieve better results."

✅ **Alternative:** "The CEO declared plans to enter new markets and boost performance."

**Why it's better:** Vivid verbs create clearer mental images and stronger prose.

### "Words Gone Wild"

**Commonly Misused Words**

These word pairs trip up even experienced writers:

- **affect/effect**: Affect (verb) = influence; Effect (noun) = result; Effect (verb) = bring about
- **imply/infer**: Imply = suggest indirectly; Infer = conclude from evidence
- **comprise/compose**: The whole comprises the parts; The parts compose the whole
- **less/fewer**: Less (uncountable); Fewer (countable)
- **further/farther**: Further (metaphorical); Farther (physical distance)

❌ **Wrong:** "The data infers a positive trend."

✅ **Correct:** "The data implies a positive trend." (or "We infer a positive trend from the data.")

---

**Inappropriate Formality ("Utilize" vs "Use")**

Some words sound more impressive but add no meaning:

- "utilize" → "use" (almost always)
- "facilitate" → "help," "enable," "ease"
- "implement" → "start," "begin," "do," "use"
- "leverage" → "use," "exploit," "apply"
- "optimize" → "improve," "enhance," "maximize"
- "strategize" → "plan," "develop strategy"

❌ **Pretentious:** "We will utilize our resources to facilitate the implementation of the optimization strategy."

✅ **Clear:** "We'll use our resources to help implement the improvement strategy."

✅ **Better:** "We'll use our resources to improve our strategy."

**Why it's better:** Simpler words are almost always clearer and more confident.

---

**Clichés and Tired Phrases**

These weaken writing by substituting thought with formula:

- "think outside the box" → be specific about innovation needed
- "at the end of the day" → "ultimately," "finally," or delete
- "low-hanging fruit" → "easy wins," "quick opportunities"
- "move the needle" → "make progress," "improve metrics"
- "game changer" → explain the actual change
- "synergy" → explain the actual benefit of collaboration
- "circle back" → "follow up," "revisit," "return to"
- "take it to the next level" → specify the improvement

❌ **Clichéd:** "At the end of the day, we need to think outside the box to move the needle on this game-changing opportunity."

✅ **Fresh:** "Ultimately, we need innovative approaches to make significant progress on this major opportunity."

✅ **Better:** "We need bold ideas to capitalize on this market opportunity."

**Why it's better:** Original phrasing shows original thought.

---

**Jargon Overuse**

Industry jargon has its place with expert audiences but alienates others:

**Business jargon:**
- "actionable insights" → "useful findings," "practical discoveries"
- "going forward" → "from now on," "in the future"
- "bandwidth" (metaphorical) → "time," "capacity," "resources"
- "reach out" → "contact," "email," "call"

**Tech jargon:**
- "seamless" → "smooth," "integrated," "unified"
- "robust" → "reliable," "powerful," "comprehensive"
- "ecosystem" → "environment," "system," "network"

Use jargon only when:
1. Your audience expects it
2. It's more precise than alternatives
3. Explaining it would take more words

❌ **Overused:** "Going forward, we need to reach out to stakeholders to get their bandwidth for actionable insights."

✅ **Clear:** "We need to contact stakeholders to find time for their practical input."

**Why it's better:** Accessible to all readers.

### Appropriateness: Matching Tone to Audience

**Formal vs. Casual**

Different contexts require different language:

**Academic/Formal:**
- Use precise terminology
- Avoid contractions
- Prefer complete sentences
- Use third person

**Business Professional:**
- Balance formality with readability
- Contractions acceptable in email, not in reports
- First and second person okay

**Marketing/Creative:**
- Conversational tone
- Contractions welcome
- Personality and voice
- Address reader directly

❌ **Wrong tone (too casual for formal report):** "The data's pretty clear that customers don't really like the old version."

✅ **Appropriate:** "The data clearly indicates customer dissatisfaction with the previous version."

---

❌ **Wrong tone (too formal for marketing email):** "We are writing to inform you that our organization has initiated a promotional campaign."

✅ **Appropriate:** "We're excited to share our latest promotion with you!"

**Why it matters:** Tone mismatch undermines credibility and connection.

## 3. Rhythm & Flow

### Sentence Length Variation

Monotonous sentence length creates dull, plodding prose. Varying length creates rhythm and emphasizes key points.

**Short sentences (3-8 words):** Create impact. Emphasize points. Demand attention.

**Medium sentences (10-20 words):** Provide the backbone of most clear, readable prose without overwhelming the reader.

**Long sentences (20+ words):** Allow you to develop complex ideas, show relationships between concepts, and create a flowing, thoughtful rhythm that carries readers through sophisticated arguments.

**The mix matters:**

❌ **Monotonous:** "Our product is innovative. Our team is experienced. Our customers are satisfied. Our prices are competitive. Our service is excellent."

✅ **Varied:** "Our product is innovative. With an experienced team and satisfied customers behind us, we're positioned for growth. Our prices remain competitive, and our service? Excellent."

**Why it's better:** The rhythm pulls readers along. The short question at the end adds emphasis.

---

❌ **All long:** "The company has been working on developing a new product line that will address customer concerns that have been raised over the past year, and we believe that this new approach will help us to better serve our market while also improving our competitive position in ways that will benefit both our customers and our shareholders."

✅ **Varied:** "For the past year, customers have raised concerns. We listened. Our new product line addresses those concerns while improving our competitive position—a win for customers and shareholders alike."

**Why it's better:** Short sentences create drama and emphasis. The dash adds a conversational aside.

### Syntactic Patterns

**Parallelism for Emphasis**

When listing items or comparing ideas, use parallel grammatical structure:

❌ **Not parallel:** "We need to increase revenue, cutting costs, and customer satisfaction should be improved."

✅ **Parallel:** "We need to increase revenue, cut costs, and improve customer satisfaction."

✅ **Alternative:** "Three goals: increase revenue, cut costs, improve customer satisfaction."

**Why it's better:** Parallel structure is easier to process and more memorable.

---

**Famous examples:**
- "We came, we saw, we conquered."
- "Government of the people, by the people, for the people."
- "Ask not what your country can do for you—ask what you can do for your country."

---

**Strategic Inversion**

Occasionally inverting normal sentence order (subject-verb-object) creates emphasis:

✅ **Normal:** "A great opportunity awaits us."

✅ **Inverted:** "Awaiting us: a great opportunity."

✅ **Normal:** "Success requires hard work, dedication, and persistence."

✅ **Inverted:** "Hard work, dedication, and persistence—these are what success requires."

**Why it works:** Inversion surprises the reader and emphasizes the inverted element.

---

**Breaking Up Long Sentences**

When a sentence grows too complex, break it:

❌ **Too complex:** "The report, which was completed last week by the research team after months of data collection and analysis, suggests that we should consider revising our strategy, though some stakeholders have expressed concerns about the timing of such changes."

✅ **Broken up:** "The research team completed the report last week after months of data collection. It suggests we revise our strategy. However, some stakeholders worry about the timing."

✅ **Alternative:** "Last week's research report—months in the making—recommends a strategy revision. Some stakeholders have timing concerns."

**Why it's better:** Readers can process one idea at a time.

### Reading Aloud Test

The best way to test rhythm: read your writing aloud.

**Listen for:**
- Places where you stumble (rewrite for clarity)
- Monotonous rhythm (vary sentence length)
- Gasping for breath (sentence too long)
- Awkward phrasing (sounds unnatural)
- Boring cadence (all sentences same pattern)

**Natural speech patterns inform good writing rhythm.** If you wouldn't say it that way, don't write it that way.

### Punctuation for Pacing

**Periods:** Full stops. Create clear boundaries. Control pacing.

**Commas:** Brief pauses, separation within sentences, but maintain connection.

**Em-dashes:** Create emphasis—or interruption—or parenthetical asides that stand out more than commas.

**Semicolons:** Link closely related independent clauses; they're stronger than commas but softer than periods.

**Colons:** Build anticipation for what follows: a list, an explanation, or an emphasized statement.

Example showing different effects:

**Version 1 (periods):** "The product failed. The team learned valuable lessons. We'll succeed next time."
- **Effect:** Staccato, definitive, emphatic

**Version 2 (commas/conjunctions):** "The product failed, but the team learned valuable lessons, and we'll succeed next time."
- **Effect:** Connected, flowing, optimistic

**Version 3 (semicolons):** "The product failed; the team learned valuable lessons; we'll succeed next time."
- **Effect:** Balanced, measured, sophisticated

**Version 4 (dash):** "The product failed—but the team learned valuable lessons that guarantee future success."
- **Effect:** Dramatic turn, emphasized learning

**Version 5 (colon):** "The product failed for one reason: we didn't test it properly. Next time will be different."
- **Effect:** Sets up explanation, forward-looking

## 4. Grammar for Effect

### Strategic Grammatical Choices

**Fragments for Emphasis**

Complete sentences aren't always necessary. Strategic fragments create impact.

✅ **With fragment:** "The competition launched first. Beat us by three months. A tough blow."

✅ **All complete:** "The competition launched first. They beat us by three months. It was a tough blow."

**Why fragments work:** Short. Punchy. Memorable. (See what we did there?)

Use sparingly for maximum effect.

---

**Em-Dashes for Interruption or Emphasis**

Em-dashes (—) are more emphatic than commas, less formal than parentheses:

✅ "The solution—when we finally found it—was surprisingly simple."

✅ "We have one goal—win."

✅ "The results were clear, decisive, and undeniable—we had succeeded."

---

**Semicolons for Closely Related Ideas**

Semicolons link independent clauses that are closely related; they show connection without subordination.

✅ "The design is elegant; the functionality is powerful; the price is right."

✅ "We tried the conventional approach; it failed; we innovated."

**When to use semicolons:**
- To link closely related complete thoughts
- In complex lists where items contain commas
- To create sophisticated rhythm

**When not to use semicolons:**
- To connect unrelated ideas (use period)
- To connect dependent clause to independent clause (use comma)
- In casual, conversational writing (they feel formal)

---

**Colons for Anticipation**

Colons signal "here it comes":

✅ "We need three things: time, money, and talent."

✅ "The verdict is clear: we must change."

✅ "Remember this: customers come first."

### Active vs. Passive Voice

**Default to active voice,** but passive has its place.

**Active (preferred most of the time):**
- Clearer
- More direct
- More concise
- More engaging

✅ "The team completed the project ahead of schedule."

**Passive (appropriate when):**

1. **Actor is unknown:**
✅ "The building was constructed in 1920." (We don't know who built it)

2. **Actor is irrelevant:**
✅ "Mistakes were made in the original calculation." (Who made them doesn't matter)

3. **Emphasis on action, not actor:**
✅ "The drug was approved by the FDA after extensive testing." (Focus: approval, not the approvers)

4. **Scientific writing convention:**
✅ "The solution was heated to 100°C." (Standard scientific style)

5. **Diplomatic softening:**
✅ "Concerns have been raised about the timeline." (Softer than "Everyone thinks the timeline is terrible")

❌ **Passive overuse:** "The report was written by the team and it was submitted to management where it was reviewed and approved and then it was distributed to all stakeholders."

✅ **Active rewrite:** "The team wrote the report, management approved it, and we distributed it to all stakeholders."

### Punctuation for Rhythm

Strategic punctuation creates rhythm and emphasis beyond just grammatical correctness.

**Periods for pace control:**
- Short sentences. Quick pace. Urgent feeling.
- Longer sentences slow things down and give readers time to process complex ideas.

**Commas for brief pauses:**
"We analyzed the data, identified the problem, and developed a solution."

**Em-dashes for longer pauses or emphasis:**
"We analyzed the data—all 10,000 rows—and found the pattern."

**Semicolons for balanced clauses:**
"The design team focused on aesthetics; the engineering team prioritized functionality; both succeeded."

**Example showing different punctuation effects:**

**Version A:** "The product launch was successful. Sales exceeded expectations. The team celebrated."
- **Effect:** Straightforward, factual, quick pace

**Version B:** "The product launch was successful, sales exceeded expectations, and the team celebrated."
- **Effect:** Connected narrative, flowing, unified event

**Version C:** "The product launch was successful; sales exceeded expectations; the team celebrated."
- **Effect:** Parallel structure, balanced, sophisticated

**Version D:** "The product launch was successful—sales exceeded expectations—and the team celebrated accordingly."
- **Effect:** Emphasized surprise at sales, formal conclusion

---

## Summary of Key Principles

1. **Eliminate sentence killers:** Conjunction chains, filler words, passive overuse, buried subjects
2. **Use strong verbs:** Replace weak verb + noun with single powerful verb
3. **Be precise:** Choose specific, vivid words over vague, generic ones
4. **Vary rhythm:** Mix short, medium, and long sentences strategically
5. **Match tone to audience:** Formal, professional, or casual as appropriate
6. **Read aloud:** If it sounds awkward, rewrite it
7. **Use punctuation for effect:** Different marks create different rhythms
8. **Break rules strategically:** Fragments, inversions, and other techniques for emphasis

The sentence is your fundamental unit. Master it, and your writing will soar.
